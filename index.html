AP Capstone

AP Research
& Seminar

"How can censorship be monitored and moderated in a way that doesn't take power away from the people?"
What I Studied
The Longstanding Issue of Censorship
I studied how censorship is governed across different systems, from authoritarian governments like North Korea to democratic frameworks like the EU's Digital Services Act, and why citizens are often consistently left out of the decisions about what gets censored and why. My focus was on the gap between who holds censorship power and who should have a voice in it.
How I Studied It
Qualitative Content Analysis
I used qualitative content analysis to examine over 20 documents across five stakeholder groups: governments, civil liberty organizations, tech companies, academics, and news media. Each document was coded for how it framed transparency, public oversight, rights, harm definitions, and who holds power. With this I used a structured codebook I built to track patterns across sources.
What I Found
A Clear Pattern in the Data
Civil liberty organizations emphasized transparency and public oversight in 8 out of 8 documents. Government documents scored 0 on both. Tech companies fell in the middle, acknowledging transparency in principle rather than building real accountability into their systems. The data confirmed that those with the most censorship power are the least likely to advocate for public involvement in it.
AP Seminar
Team Research & Presentation
AP Seminar was the foundation for AP Research, as it taught me how to analyze complex problems from multiple perspectives, build evidence-based arguments, and present research clearly. Working in teams and individually on presentations built the skills I used throughout my research paper on censorship.
